  • Why does $I(t)$ peak and go down?

In the case of Infected people$(I)$, the number of infected people initially increases, and then decreases given the SIR Model.

The reason the number of infected people increases is because transmission occurs through contact between infected people$(I)$ and susceptible people$(S)$.

That is, when an infected person$(I)$ comes into contact with an susceptible person$(S)$, there is a certain probability $p$ that the susceptible person$(S)$ becomes infected.

where $\beta$ represents “transmission rate”. If contact occurs a lot, the number of infected people increases.

But as the number of infected people increases, the total number of susceptible people decreases. Because the number of susceptible people that an infected person can infect by contacting decreases

To consider change infected people according to susceptible people, change in infected people compared to non-infected people

Denote $R_0 = \frac{\beta}{\mu}$, then

Hence, even if the number of infected$(I)$ people increases, contact with susceptible$(S)$ people decreases. As a result, the growth rate of the number of infected people decreases.


  • Why is there a residual $S$?

In the SIR model, $S$ represents the population without immunity to the disease.

When infectious disease spreads, contact between $S$ and $I$ increases. Therefore $I$ increases and $S$ decreases over time. But # of $S$ decreases, the change of $I (= dI/dt)$ also slow, too. It means the spread of infectious disease appears to be suppressed.

Therefore, the residual remains after $S$ is decreased.
